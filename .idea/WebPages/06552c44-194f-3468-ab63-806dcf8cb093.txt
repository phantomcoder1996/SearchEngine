           <p><i><b><a href="/wiki/The_Age_of_Reason" title="The Age of Reason">The Age of Reason</a></b></i> is a work by English and American political activist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Paine" title="Thomas Paine">Thomas Paine</a> <i>(pictured)</i>, arguing for the philosophical position of <a href="/wiki/Deism" title="Deism">deism</a>. Following in the tradition of eighteenth-century British deism, it challenges institutionalized religion and the legitimacy of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>. It was published in three parts in 1794, 1795, and 1807, and became a best-seller in the United States, where it caused a short-lived deistic revival. Fearing its revolutionary ideas, the British government prosecuted printers and book-sellers who tried to publish and distribute it. <i>The Age of Reason</i> highlights what Paine saw as corruption among <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> churches and criticizes their efforts to acquire political power. Paine advocates reason over <a href="/wiki/Revelation" title="Revelation">revelation</a>, leading him to reject <a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">miracles</a> and to view the Bible as an ordinary piece of literature rather than as a divinely inspired text. He promotes <a href="/wiki/Natural_theology" title="Natural theology">natural religion</a> and argues for the existence of a creator-God. Most of Paine's arguments had long been available to educated people, but his engaging, irreverent and inexpensive <a href="/wiki/Pamphlet" title="Pamphlet">pamphlets</a> made deism appealing and accessible to a mass audience. (<a href="/wiki/The_Age_of_Reason" title="The Age of Reason"><b>Full&nbsp;article...</b></a>)</p>

        <div id="mp-tfl" style="padding:0.3em 0.7em;">
         American singer 
         <b><a href="/wiki/Selena_singles_discography" title="Selena singles discography">Selena released</a></b> twenty-seven official 
         <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">singles</a>, seven 
         <a href="/wiki/Promotional_recording" title="Promotional recording">promotional singles</a>, and made five 
         <a href="/wiki/Guest_vocalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Guest vocalist">guest vocalist</a> appearances. Her career began as the lead vocalist of 
         <a href="/wiki/Selena_y_Los_Dinos" title="Selena y Los Dinos">Los Dinos</a> in 1980. In 1987, her cover of 
         <a href="/wiki/Ritchie_Valens" title="Ritchie Valens">Ritchie Valens</a>' "
         <a href="/wiki/La_Bamba_(song)" title="La Bamba (song)">La Bamba</a>" peaked at number 19 on the United States 
         <a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)"><i>Billboard</i></a> 
         <a href="/wiki/Hot_Latin_Songs" title="Hot Latin Songs">Hot Latin Songs</a> chart, her first entry. She signed with 
         <a href="/wiki/EMI_Latin" class="mw-redirect" title="EMI Latin">EMI Latin</a> nine years later as a solo artist though her band continued to tour with her. Selena appeared on "
         <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Amigos" title="Buenos Amigos">Buenos Amigos</a>" with Salvadoran singer 
         <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_Torres" title="Álvaro Torres">Álvaro Torres</a>. The track peaked at number one on the U.S. Hot Latin Songs chart in 1991, the singer's first number one song. Selena's live recording entitled 
         <i><a href="/wiki/Selena_Live!" title="Selena Live!">Live</a></i> (1993) contained three studio tracks and produced two top five U.S. singles, "
         <a href="/wiki/No_Debes_Jugar" title="No Debes Jugar">No Debes Jugar</a>" and "
         <a href="/wiki/La_Llamada" title="La Llamada">La Llamada</a>". After her collaboration with the 
         <a href="/wiki/Barrio_Boyzz" title="Barrio Boyzz">Barrio Boyzz</a> on the single "
         <a href="/wiki/Donde_Quiera_Que_Est%C3%A9s" title="Donde Quiera Que Estés">Donde Quiera Que Estés</a>" peaked at number one on the Hot Latin Songs chart, Selena released her fourth 
         <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" class="mw-redirect" title="Studio album">studio album</a> 
         <i><a href="/wiki/Amor_Prohibido" title="Amor Prohibido">Amor Prohibido</a></i> (1994). The album continued the singer's streak of US number one singles with "
         <a href="/wiki/Amor_Prohibido_(song)" title="Amor Prohibido (song)">Amor Prohibido</a>", "
         <a href="/wiki/Bidi_Bidi_Bom_Bom" title="Bidi Bidi Bom Bom">Bidi Bidi Bom Bom</a>", "
         <a href="/wiki/No_Me_Queda_M%C3%A1s" title="No Me Queda Más">No Me Queda Más</a>", and

         <a href="/wiki/Fotos_y_Recuerdos" title="Fotos y Recuerdos">Fotos y Recuerdos</a>". The latter peaked at number one posthumously following the 
         <a href="/wiki/Murder_of_Selena" title="Murder of Selena">shooting death</a> of Selena on March 31, 1995. (

            <td style="padding:0 6px 0 0"> <p><i><b><a href="/wiki/The_Kiss_(Munch_painting)" title="The Kiss (Munch painting)">The Kiss</a></b></i> is an <a href="/wiki/Oil_painting" title="Oil painting">oil painting</a> on <a href="/wiki/Canvas" title="Canvas">canvas</a> completed by the Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Symbolism_(arts)" title="Symbolism (arts)">symbolist</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Edvard_Munch" title="Edvard Munch">Edvard Munch</a> in 1897. Exhibited as early as 1903, this work is held at the <a href="/wiki/Munch_Museum" title="Munch Museum">Munch Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>.</p> <p><small>Painting: <a href="/wiki/Edvard_Munch" title="Edvard Munch">Edvard Munch</a></small></p>
